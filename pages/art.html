Intersectionality in Manet’s Famous Olympia

Olympia by Édouard Manet emerges as a compelling site for examining how works of art can articulate inequality within a single visual system. In this painting, issues of gender, race, and class coexist quite explicitly, yet they are often interpreted in isolation. Intersectionality, as theorised by Crenshaw, allows the painting to be read more fully by showing how these structures work together rather than separately.

Hierarchy

Olympia’s centrality depends on the maid’s marginalisation. The painting constructs power relationally, not independently.

Femininity

The painting produces two different femininities—one sexualised and one labouring—through race and class.

Visibility

The work organises attention unevenly, making whiteness instantly legible while Blackness is pushed toward the edge of perception.

First, the composition constructs hierarchy relationally. Olympia, reclining, nude, and confrontational, occupies the centre through contrast with the Black maid, who remains clothed and recedes into the background. Spatial and luminous oppositions—centre and periphery, light and shadow—encode gender, race, and class simultaneously. Olympia’s centrality is produced through the maid’s racialised and labouring marginalisation, revealing inequality as a system of interdependent relations rather than as a set of isolated categories.

Second, the painting stages two differentiated forms of femininity whose meanings only emerge in relation to one another. Olympia’s body is sexualised, frontal, and economically legible, while the maid’s body is functional, desexualised, and visually subordinated. This contrast does not simply oppose two women. It structures femininity itself along racial lines, making gender inseparable from race. Olympia’s desirability and limited agency are produced through the presence of a racialised figure assigned to service and partial invisibility.

Another important aspect is the way the painting articulates two interconnected economies of the body. Olympia participates in a sexual economy in which her body is directly exchanged, looked at, desired, and rewarded. The maid, by contrast, is confined to domestic labour and facilitates that exchange from the margins. The bouquet she carries passes through her hands and makes her the material intermediary of Olympia’s desirability.

The painting also establishes a visual economy of attention: not all bodies are equally visible to the viewer. Rather than simply opposing light and shadow, it regulates how quickly and fully each figure can be apprehended. Olympia is immediately legible, while the maid requires longer and more careful looking. This uneven visibility is structural. Whiteness becomes the natural site of attention, while Blackness is rendered peripheral, supporting but not competing with the central figure. Visibility itself becomes a function of inequality.

Olympia confronts the viewer directly, asserting a form of controlled visibility, while the maid remains outside the primary circuit of the gaze. This asymmetry implicates the viewer. To look at Olympia is to participate in a visual transaction that excludes the maid even as her presence sustains the scene. The gaze is therefore not neutral; it is distributed across unequal positions and organised by power.

Finally, the painting must be situated within an imperial and colonial context. The intersecting hierarchies it stages belong to a broader circulation of bodies, labour, and representation shaped by nineteenth-century French colonialism. What appears as an intimate interior is structured by larger global relations of power. Race, class, and gender are not only co-present in the painting—they are historically produced together.

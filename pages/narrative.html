A History of Two Zebras

A short narrative about friendship, school, and the quiet ways that institutions sort bodies into different histories.

Tayo and RyryNarrative mode

In another world, there are two groups of zebras: one with white skin beneath black stripes and the other with black skin beneath white stripes. To the naked eye, they look exactly the same without careful inspection. Tayo and Ryry are two young foals, one from each group, and neither knows of the hidden biological differences beneath their fur.

A History of Two Zebras

By the river, they are two friends playing in the mud. They spend their mornings chasing butterflies through the tall reeds, splashing in the cool water, and drying off under the delighted sun. When they look down at the river surface, they see only two black-and-white zebras side by side. They run together every day with children’s innocence, racing to the old willow tree. Yet the cheerful rhythm of those mornings begins to change once they enter school.

On their first morning at Valley Primary School, Tayo and Ryry wait at the front office to collect their class schedules. The busy clerk asks for their names. When Ryry speaks, the clerk opens a dark-green registry book and ticks off his name. When Tayo speaks, the clerk pauses, searches through another list, and eventually pulls out a different, thinner registry book with a grey cover. He marks Tayo’s name there instead.

“Why are we in different books?” Tayo asks, stretching his neck to see into the drawer.

The clerk does not look up. “It’s just administrative policy,” he says casually. “Different backgrounds are filed in different records. It’s just how the school has always organised things. Next in line, please.”

Ryry shrugs, half-sympathetic but eager to get to class. Tayo feels a strange knot tighten in his chest, though he cannot yet name it.

Inside the classroom, things continue to feel different in ways Tayo cannot fully explain. During history class, the teacher hands out a heavy book called The Great Valley Journey. She points to an illustration of a proud herd running across a bright open field.

“This is how our ancestors found this land and made it safe,” she says. She asks Ryry to read the first page aloud. He reads a story about zebras crossing a raging river. When he finishes, the teacher smiles and tells the class that they should admire the excellent qualities of their ancestors.

Tayo looks closely at the picture and the map beside it. It does not match the bedtime stories his grandmother tells him. In those stories, zebras do not cross a river. They travel through dark mountains and harsh winds to reach the same sunny field. He raises his hoof and asks whether that story is also in the book.

The teacher frowns slightly. “Yes, Tayo. There were some who came another way, but we focus on this group today. It will be on the test. Let’s stick to the main part.”

Later, while walking down the hallway to get a drink of water, Tayo is stopped by a hall monitor who asks suspiciously where he is supposed to be. He has just seen Ryry walk through the same hallway without question.

That afternoon, Tayo walks home in silence. He does not race Ryry to the willow tree. Instead, he goes straight inside and sits beside his grandmother, who is mending a blanket by the window. He asks her why his name was written in a different book and why the school’s history does not match the story she has told him.

His grandmother sets the blanket down and looks at him for a long moment.

“You are all young zebras, Tayo,” she says gently. “But the valley has its own habits. They sort you by the old records they kept in drawers, and by rules that were made long before you were born. You are the same, but recorded in different ways. There isn’t our story in the schoolbook because it isn’t their history—it is ours. They only crossed the river, so they never learned to record the mountains.”

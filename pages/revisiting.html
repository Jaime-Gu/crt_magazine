Color-Blind Racism

In 2003, Eduardo Bonilla-Silva published a foundational work in the field of critical race theory entitled Racism Without Racists: Color-Blind Racism and the Persistence of Racial Inequality in the United States. In the book, Bonilla-Silva analysed a myriad of interviews—many with college students—to pursue an answer to a simple question: what does racism look like in the post-civil rights United States?

Though Bonilla-Silva’s book was pivotal in both critical race theory and critical whiteness studies, 2003 was two decades ago. Bonilla-Silva had no way of knowing that the country he was writing about would undergo another political transformation; that the sociopolitical sphere and the ways it interacts with race, racism, and intersectionality would shift again. Gay marriage would be legalised, America would elect its first Black president, the Me Too movement would rise to prominence, and the BLM movement would take on new visibility following George Floyd’s murder. At the same time, the ideological pendulum would swing to the right, and anti-immigrant and anti-DEI rhetoric would become increasingly central to public debate.

The playing field has been resown; the goalposts have been moved. Society has changed, but perhaps the most glaring fact is that, in some ways, it has not. Looking back at Racism Without Racists, the book does not feel dated. Instead, it feels prescient.

The conversations Bonilla-Silva began in 2003—though transformed by the present—remain deeply relevant. What follows are interviews with contemporary college students, aiming to revisit and reframe those earlier conversations within the sociopolitical climate of the 2020s.

Society has changed, but perhaps the most glaring fact is that, in some ways, it has not.

I’m a liberal, but…

Performative liberalism on college campuses is hardly new. College has long been a space where individual social rebellion is presented as political leaning. What has grown in more recent times, however, is liberalism expressed as a base coat for further political ideologies. Many respondents felt the need to assure the interviewer that they were, in fact, liberal before articulating contradictory opinions.

Q: What does racism look like on your college campus?
Student: “You know… I’m a liberal and everything, but I really don’t think we have much of it at our school. Of course you’ll have one or two weird people, but for the most part it’s not really a thing anymore.”

These declarations of liberalism appear to reassure the interviewer that the speaker is socially aware, yet they frequently precede arguably illiberal ideas. Here, racism is pronounced to be essentially over. It is written off as the problem of “one or two” fringe individuals, rather than as a structural condition.

In this framing, racism is neither systemic nor the result of hatred or implicit bias. Instead, it is reduced to the behaviour of “weird people,” discussed more as a personality flaw than as part of institutional life.

Q: So does that mean racism isn’t an issue anymore?
Student: “Well… again, I’m a liberal and I vote Democrat and all, but I just really don’t think it’s cool. People don’t like racism. It’s not the norm anymore.”

Once again, assurances of liberalism and acceptable voting patterns cover a strange discussion of racism as being merely “uncool.” Racism is treated in the same register as popularity or social fashion. In this view, racism belongs to the world of obvious bullies and overt cruelty, not to the quieter structures of everyday inequality. Once racism slips from the overt to the covert, it becomes easier not to acknowledge it at all.

I’m all for equality…

Though much has changed since Bonilla-Silva’s book, one issue has remained firmly in the sociopolitical zeitgeist: affirmative action. Repackaged within the language of diversity, equity, and inclusion, it remains a hot-button issue on which white students often invoke words like “fairness,” “merit,” and “reverse racism.”

Q: Do you think universities should implement an affirmative action policy in their admission process?
Student: “I think people should be accepted based on their qualifications—or their merits. On the other side of things, I don’t think I should be penalised just because I’m white.”
Q: Do you feel that you’ve been penalised because of your race?
Student: “Well, no… I was speaking in the hypothetical. No students should be rejected because they’re White.”

At the end of the day, these are smart students who understand the ideological necessity for some form of remedial response to educational inequality. Yet that recognition is pushed aside in favour of a conversation about hypothetical penalties to whiteness. The debate becomes less about uplift and more about imagined loss.

Bonilla-Silva describes the rhetorical strategies of color-blind racism. One strategy visible here is intentional misunderstanding: a deliberate recalibration of the conversation toward false equivalence. Affirmative action becomes framed not as remediation, but as punishment.

These interviews are not intended to expose a hidden population of closet racists on college campuses. Racism exists after slavery, after the civil rights movement, and after Barack Obama’s election as president. It has not disappeared; it has evolved.

These conversations illuminate that evolution. Political debate has grown more nuanced and commonplace in everyday life, and so too has color-blind racism. It may now be better described as racism-blind racism.

Note

The interviews in this article are fictitious and are intended for narrative and educational effect only.
